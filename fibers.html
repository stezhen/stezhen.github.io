Fibers

Search Engine for Accessible Clothing

Overview

Client

Student Project for Medium Design Collective

Problem

How can we simultaneously promote the diversity and inclusion of bodies?

Solution

FIBERS is a search engine for people with specific needs when looking for clothing. Our mission is to create a special and memorable experience when finding clothes, tailored to meet their personal needs.

Research

Task Analysis

Based on research conducted online and in-person interviews, I first focused on defining the problem space for mobility disabilities. Then, as a group, we pieced together our individual insights to synthesize a larger and more general problem statement analysis.

In doing so, we narrowed down the scope to clothing because everybody needs it and the body directly impacts clothing decisions.

My First Problem Statement Analysis

Team's First Problem Statement Analysis

Interviews

While conducting interviews for defining the problem space, I discovered:

  1. People generally do not know what they want

  2. Fashion is rooted in ableism

  3. Companies and designers can be afraid of specially designing for specific bodies out of the fear that their designs will face backlash for inaccuracy.

  4. The concept of disabilities is created and perpetuated by society

When conducting interviews to design the solution, I discovered:

  1. Many solutions focus on an “empathetic model” and not on empowerment, which can further alienate people with disabilities

  2. Many major labels have disability clothing lines but they are not well-known and advertised

  3. Narrow Down Specific Student Groups to Problem Solve for

  4. The difference between Blind Disabilities vs. Visible Disabilities

  5. Disabilities affect how someone interacts with people and the environment

User Personas

Using research and interview insights, I defined two sample target groups: Mary, a working mother trying to buy adaptive clothing for her son, and Sam, a man with MS looking to buy adaptive clothing for himself. These user personas allow me to empathize with the user’s story and humanize their needs for the design.

First Iteration

Brainstorming

Our first solution had two aspects, a physical exhibition for the annual Medium Raw Exp and a functional website. The Physical Exhibition would be used to demonstrate what the website does in real life and give the user an “in-person experience”. The exhibit would raise awareness for the issue in our community and the website would display the solution.

I focused on the exhibition experience and narrowed down the solution to providing solvency and awareness for dexterity and prosthetic disabilities, sizing issues, and orthotic footwear.

2D - Rendering

As a low-fidelity prototype for the exhibit, I created 2D-Renderings on my iPad to show the storymap.

An Overview of the Exhibit

The Experience

Step 1
Try On Clothing

Visitors of the Exhibit try on the clothing from the mannequin which will demonstrate the difficulty that people who have those disabilities experience when dressing themselves. There will be mirrors in a "photobooth" setting so that people can take pictures with friends and raise awareness for the exhibit and its cause.

Goal 1: Raise Awareness
Understand how it feels to be in someone else’s shoes
Realize the need for inclusive design of diverse bodies

Goal 2: Spread the word
Mirrors set up for taking photos with friends
Take pictures of the clothing and share on social media


Part 2:
Connection to the Website

Next, visitors can find the solutions to the problem by connecting to the website through the QR code copied onto each mirror.

Goal 1: Shows the importance for the website
Provides solvency

Goal 2: Increases website traffic and exposure
Easy and effective way to spread the word


Dexterity Clothing

Dexterity
Part 1: Buttons

The button up shirt would be fastened with small buttons to exaggerate the dressing process for an able-bodied individual.

Buttoning requires a sufficient amount of bilateral coordination skills to complete the task.


Dexterity
Part 2: Zippers

Using a smaller and difficult zipper replicates the frustration that someone with dexterity issues experiences.

Zippers, snaps, buckles are all fasteners that require finger dexterity and complete motor skills to open and close. Efficient control of the larger muscle groups in the neck, shoulder and trunk is necessary to maintain stability in order to place the fingers and hands in the proper position to zip/unzip, snap and buckle.


Goal of the Dexterity Models:

  1. Demonstrate how difficult and frustrating fastening buttons or zipping up a jacket can be for someone with dexterity issues.

  2. Grow demand for including and popularizing adaptive clothing

Sizing Clothing

Sizing
Part 1: "Petite"

The petite clothing will be placed on an adjustable mannequin. Because it plays on the stereotype of smaller, shorter, and tighter clothing, visitors will be able to see the lack of inclusivity.

As an example, somebody with dwarfism may technically fall into the petite category but cannot wear the clothes or must alter it significantly because it does not account for their height.


Sizing
Part 2: "Plus Size"

The plus size clothing will be placed on an adjustable mannequin. When the mannequin is expanded, this will demonstrate how traditional plus size clothing will not fit as expected on plus size people because brands tend to use a straight-size model instead of a plus size model. In addition, it will emphasize the plus size clothing stereotype of having a frumpy style, cheap fabric, and poorly cut edges.

Brands in a rush to jump the size-inclusivity bandwagon, falling short of many expectations. This fails because weight shifts as you gain weight.


Goal of the Sizing Models:

  1. Discourage lumping people with petite sizes and plus sizing as "special size customers."

  2. Encourage utilizing anthropometrical measurements to promote inclusion.

  3. Promote the incorporation of the in-betweens on the spectrum of the extremes

Orthopetic Footwear

Orthopetic Footwear

The deconstructed orthopedic shoe will be separated layer by layer to demonstrate some of the many specifications that someone with cerebral palsy, spina bifida, or a brain injury need.

Some of these specifications include:

  • stronger material to prevent being chewed up by orthotics

  • a wider heel pocket to fit orthotics

  • velcro or an extra ankle wrap

  • and high-tech shock absorbing materials

Shoes are a necessity because they express a freedom in movement and functionality.

Goal: Shows able-bodied individuals the difficulty in finding shoes that fit their needs.


Feedback

Our Presentation

Our group then recieved feedback at our club, Medium Design Collective's first critique with four Cornell Professors and approximately 50 attendees.

Key Insights:

  1. Focus on either awareness or clothing finding

  2. Ensure the try-on experience does not essentializing the experience of the disabled

  3. Focus on one body type and disability first - trying to provide solvency for multiple problems at once is too difficult.


Second Iteration

Brainstorming

The exhibit was still too broad of a focus and disconnected to the website. So, we decided to go back to basics and conduct more research. In addition, I realized the exhibit might have a negative connotation by invoking pity for disabled people instead of empowering them.

We decided to narrow our scope, cutting out the exhibit entirely and focusing only on the website to provide solvency.

User Flow

We mapped out the structure using a user flow diagram to map out the search process.

Low-Fidelity Prototype

We planned out a very simple low-fidelity prototype on a chalkboard to determine what the user flow would look like in the context of a website.

Home page, popup, questionnaire, search results, item details page

Final Result

Our finished product was a clickable prototype on Figma. We had planned to put it into testing. With Covid-19, however, our club's activities were disrupted.
