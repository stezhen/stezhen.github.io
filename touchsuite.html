Research

Task Analysis

Based on research conducted online and in-person interviews, I first focused on defining the problem space for mobility disabilities. Then, as a group, we pieced together our individual insights to synthesize a larger and more general problem statement analysis.

In doing so, we narrowed down the scope to clothing because everybody needs it and the body directly impacts clothing decisions.

Team's First Problem Statement Analysis

Interviews

While conducting interviews for defining the problem space, I discovered:

1. People generally do not know what they want
2. Fashion is rooted in ableism
3. Companies and designers can be afraid of specially designing for specific bodies out of the fear that their designs will face backlash for inaccuracy or even tokenization.
4. The concept of disabilities is created and perpetuated by society

When conducting interviews to design the solution, I discovered:

5. Many solutions focus on an “empathetic model” and not on empowerment, which can further alienate people with disabilities.
6. Many major labels have disability clothing lines but they are not well-known and advertised.
7. Narrow Down Specific Student Groups to Problem Solve for.
8. The difference between Blind Disabilities vs. Visible Disabilities
9. Disabilities affect how someone interacts with people and the environment

User Personas

Using research and interview insights, I defined two sample target groups: Mary, a working mother trying to buy adaptive clothing for her son, and Sam, a man with MS looking to buy adaptive clothing for himself. These user personas allow me to empathize with the user’s story and humanize their needs for the design.

First Iteration

Brainstorming

Our first solution had two aspects, a physical exhibition for the annual Medium Raw Exp and a functional website. The Physical Exhibition would be used to demonstrate what the website does in real life and give the user an “in-person experience”. The exhibit would raise awareness for the issue in our community and the website would display the solution.

I focused on the exhibition experience and narrowed down the solution to providing solvency and awareness for dexterity and prosthetic disabilities, sizing issues, and orthotic footwear.

2D - Rendering

As a low-fidelity prototype for the exhibit, I created 2D-Renderings on my iPad to show the storymap.

Feedback

Our Presentation

Our group then recieved feedback at our club, Medium Design Collective's first critique with four Cornell Professors and approximately 50 attendees.

Key Insights:

1. Focus on either awareness or clothing finding
2. Ensure the try-on experience is not essentializing the experience of the disabled.
3. Trying to provide solvency for multiple problems at once is too difficult. Focus on one body type and disability first.


Second Iteration

Brainstorming

We realized, however, that this was still too broad of a focus and there was still an inherent disconnect between the website and the exhibit. So, we decided to go back to basics and conduct more research.

We decided to narrow our scope, cutting out the exhibit entirely and focusing only on providing solvency for finding adaptive clothing with the website.

User Flow

We mapped out the structure using a user flow diagram to map out the search process.

Low-Fidelity Prototype

We planned out a very simple low-fidelity prototype on a chalkboard to determine what the user flow would look like in the context of a website.

Home page, popup, questionnaire, search results, item details page

Final Result

Our finished product was a clickable prototype on Figma. We had planned to put it into testing. With Covid-19, however, our club's activities were disrupted.
